Ask just 3 questions

1

Who will wear it?

Team, event staff, giveaway audience, field force, sports group, premium merch.

2

How often will they wear it?

One event, weekly, or daily. This changes fabric, GSM, and print choice fast.

3

How many pieces + what artwork?

Quantity, logo size, colour count, and placement usually decide the print method.

1-line retail rule

Don’t sell GSM first. Sell comfort, durability, and look first.

How to structure the conversation

Retail flow: outcome → garment → branding

Step 1

Purpose

Uniform, event, promo, gifting, sports, premium merch.

Step 2

Fabric

Cotton for softness, P/C for balance, polyester for performance.

Step 3

Weight & build

GSM, knit, collar, rib, seams, wash-prep.

Step 4

Branding method

DTG, DTF, screen, print & cut, embroidery.

Step 5

Reality check

Budget, timeline, size mix, wash care, logo placement.

The staff should sound like this

“For daily team wear, I’d suggest a 180–200 GSM cotton or poly-cotton tee. It feels good, holds shape better, and your logo will still look solid after repeat use.”

Then only go deeper if needed:

“Cotton is softer”
“P/C lasts longer”
“Dry-fit is for sweat”
“Higher GSM feels more premium”

Extra factors teams forget

Fit, collar shape, sleeve finish, shrinkage control, colourfastness, size spread, and washcare are often what the customer notices later.

Training objective: the team should translate apparel tech into customer language, not dump manufacturing jargon.

Fabric basics

What fabric to recommend, and when

Cotton

Best for comfort

  • Soft, breathable, easier everyday wear
  • Works well for office teams, events, lifestyle merch
  • Feels more natural on skin in Indian weather
  • Watch-outs: can wrinkle and may shrink if untreated
Poly-cotton (P/C blend)

Best for balance

  • Blends cotton comfort with polyester durability
  • Better shape retention for daily-use uniforms
  • Great for SMB team wear and polos
  • Usually the safest “all-rounder” recommendation
Polyester / dry-fit

Best for performance

  • Quick dry, moisture-wicking, light
  • Right choice for sports, field use, outdoor events
  • Often less “premium soft” than cotton
  • Customer must care more about function than natural feel
Customer words:
soft, breathable → cotton
Safe default:
durable + decent comfort → P/C
Activity wear:
sweat, speed dry → polyester
Polo structure:
collar hold improves with heavier builds
Don’t oversell:
no single fabric is “best” for all jobs

What to say about GSM

GSM = grams per square meter. Higher GSM usually means a heavier, less flimsy garment. It does not automatically mean “better” — it means a different feel and use case.

Light = cheaper / easier for events
Mid = safest daily wear
Heavy = more premium structure

SPI / Stitch per inch

SPI tells you how dense the stitches are. Too low and seams open early. Too high and fabric can pucker or go stiff. For retail training, the team only needs this rule:

7–8 SPI = basic / okay
8–10 SPI = good retail sweet spot
10–12 SPI = more tailored / fine finish

Construction details worth noticing

Neck rib should recover, not wave out
Side seams should sit straight, not twist
Polo placket should lie flat
Shoulder seams should look even and secure
No skipped stitches or loose thread tails
Body and sleeve panels should align cleanly

Finish and quality

What “good finishing” looks like on the garment

Cutting accuracy
Panels match, sleeves look balanced, hem is level.
Stitching quality
Straight seams, even tension, no skipped stitches.
Collar / placket
Flat, aligned, buttons secure, placket not curling.
Embellishments
Buttons, labels, reinforcements, and trims feel intentional.

Retail quality checklist

No visible puckering near side seams or placket
Print area is flat and correctly positioned
Collar points are symmetric
Sleeve openings look equal on left and right
No twisting after a light tug test
Branding looks intentional, not “stuck on”
Customer translation

“This one feels cleaner and more put together.”

This is the minimum language the store team should use when checking garment readiness and perceived quality.

Branding methods

Which print type to recommend, and why

DTG

Best for: small runs, full-colour art, personalised pieces.

Tell customer: soft feel, great for complex artwork, not the cheapest at scale.

Screen print

Best for: larger quantities and bold logos.

Tell customer: durable and economical when quantity goes up.

Print & cut

Best for: names, numbers, strong flat graphics.

Tell customer: clean edges and colour pop, but a more “applied” feel.

DTF transfer

Best for: lower MOQs across multiple fabrics.

Tell customer: flexible and practical, good middle ground for SMB orders.

Embroidery

Best for: polos, uniforms, premium logos.

Tell customer: textured, durable, and more corporate-looking.

Rule of thumb
Small qty + complex artwork → DTG / DTF
Bold logo + bigger order
Screen print usually wins
Uniform / formal look
Embroidery usually wins
Print method should be sold as a function of order size, artwork style, and expected lifespan — not as a technical flex.

Recommendation playbook

Five common SMB scenarios and the default answer

Startup team tee

Recommend: 180–200 GSM cotton or P/C round neck + DTF / screen depending qty.

Why: Feels good enough to wear often, budget still controlled.

Retail / restaurant polo

Recommend: 200–260 GSM polo + embroidery.

Why: Better structure, smarter look, branding survives repeat wash.

Event giveaway

Recommend: 160–180 GSM cotton tee + screen or DTF.

Why: Keeps total cost sane for volume orders.

Field or outdoor team

Recommend: 150–180 GSM dry-fit polyester + DTF / sublimation-style logic where suitable.

Why: Function beats softness here.

Premium founder merch

Recommend: 200+ GSM cotton tee or premium polo + embroidery / premium print.

Why: Premium feel comes from structure and finish, not just the logo.

Employee joining / gifting kit

Recommend: Cotton or polo based on brand vibe, keep colours classic, avoid overly experimental garments.

Why: Safe, wearable, high perceived value.

If the team remembers only one thing: match the garment to actual use, not just customer budget.

Printo baseline

Suggested internal quality standard for retail selling

Recommended garment baseline

  • Round neck team tees: usually start at ~180 GSM for better confidence
  • Daily-use polos: 200–260 GSM depending fabric and use case
  • Sportswear: ~150–180 GSM dry-fit is okay if performance is the priority
  • Pre-shrunk / bio-washed where applicable for better post-wash experience

Construction + finish baseline

  • Good retail SPI target: roughly 8–10 for most casual apparel conversations
  • Clean seams, no skipped stitches, no twisting, no placket curl
  • Print alignment and placement checked before dispatch
  • Buttons, trims, and embroidery edges should feel secure and neat

What staff should never say

  • “This is premium because it is heavy.”
  • “Cotton is always better.”
  • “Any logo works in embroidery.”
  • “All prints last the same.”

What staff should say instead

  • “This one feels more structured.”
  • “This fabric suits daily wear better.”
  • “This logo will reproduce cleaner in this method.”
  • “This option should hold up better after repeat wash.”
This is a retail-friendly baseline, not a universal factory spec sheet. Adjust by product category, price point, and customer intent.

The deck in one sentence

Recommend apparel by use case first. Use fabric, GSM, stitching, finish, and print type only to justify the recommendation.

Simple sales script

1. “Who will wear it and how often?”

2. “Do you want soft, durable, or sporty?”

3. “How many pieces and what kind of logo?”

4. “Cool — I’d recommend this garment and this branding method.”
